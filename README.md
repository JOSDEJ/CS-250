# CS-250
How do I interpret user needs and implement them into a program? How does creating "user stories" help with this?
Interpreting user needs starts with direct engagement through interviews and conversations that surface what users actually struggle with, not just what they say they want. On the SNHU Travel project, that process revealed that budget filtering was a far higher priority than initially assumed; users kept describing the same frustration of finding a destination they liked only to discover everything was out of their price range. A user story captures that need in a structured, human-centered format: as a user, I want [feature], so that [benefit]. But the real work happens in refining the acceptance criteria. A story like "users can filter by budget" is too vague to build or test. Adding specificity (results sorted by price, filter updates in under two seconds, no full page reload) transforms a wish into a verifiable deliverable. User stories help because they keep the focus on the user's problem rather than the technical solution, and they create a shared language between developers, testers, and stakeholders.
How do I approach developing programs? What Agile processes do I hope to incorporate into my future development work?
I approach development by reading existing code before touching it, identifying exactly what needs to change and what should stay the same, and surfacing uncertainty early rather than making assumptions. The SNHU Travel project reinforced that disciplined communication is just as important as technical skill; when updated slideshow requirements arrived mid-sprint, the right move was to ask clarifying questions before writing a line of code. The Agile practices I want to carry forward include Daily Scrums for early blocker detection, backlog refinement so stories are clear before Sprint Planning, and Sprint Retrospectives that produce at least one concrete process change. These are not bureaucratic overhead; they are the structures that make it possible to respond to change without losing momentum.
What does it mean to be a good team member in software development?
Being a good team member means making your work legible to others and surfacing problems early enough for them to actually be addressed. On the SNHU Travel project, that looked different depending on the role: the tester reviewed acceptance criteria before development began and wrote test cases specific enough for another person to reproduce; the developer flagged a mismatched image filename rather than working around it; the Scrum Master redirected informal scope requests through the backlog instead of letting them quietly expand the sprint. What connected all of those behaviors was a shared commitment to keeping the team's commitments real. A good team member does not just complete their own tasks; they actively reduce the uncertainty and ambiguity that make other people's tasks harder
